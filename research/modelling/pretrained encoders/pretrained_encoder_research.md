# Pretrained Audio Encoders for OBRUXO’s Timbre Stack

## What the encoder actually has to solve

OBRUXO is not trying to do generic music understanding. The repo defines a narrower inverse problem: infer a valid Vital patch from **exactly one reference audio clip**, with **no MIDI or other conditioning at inference time**, while making **perceptual similarity in rendered audio** the primary success criterion. Just as importantly, the representation should capture **synthesis identity rather than the observed performance**, and the repo explicitly says performance invariance should be tested by re-rendering source and predicted patches under held-out performances. The same tracker also makes clear that cross-attention is only a current working default, not a central research commitment. citeturn0view0turn0view1

That framing changes the encoder search substantially. A good OBRUXO timbre encoder should preserve oscillator/filter/envelope/effects character, remain stable across note choice and phrasing, expose usable hidden states for lightweight adapters, and be practical enough to run inside a musician-facing local pipeline. Under your stated constraints, that pushes the first POC away from ultra-specialized pitch models and away from giant multimodal/audio-chat towers, and toward **general-but-music-relevant pretrained encoders** that can be frozen and interrogated layer-wise. citeturn0view0turn19view0

A broader lesson from music-SSL literature also supports that bias: when speech-oriented SSL designs are adapted to music, training on music helps, but the authors still report limitations of speech-first designs, especially for **polyphonic** information. In parallel, MARBLE’s benchmark paper reports that recently proposed **large-scale pretrained musical language models** perform best on many music tasks. That does not prove they are best for “audio clip → synth patch,” but it strongly argues that OBRUXO should start from **music-pretrained** encoders before falling back to generic speech/audio encoders. citeturn27search0turn27search1turn16view1

## Which encoder families fit this problem

### Music-native self-supervised encoders

This is the most promising family for OBRUXO’s timbre stack. The leading open candidates are **MERT**, **MusicFM**, and **MuQ**. They are all pretrained on music rather than speech-only or broad environmental audio, and all expose hidden representations suitable for frozen-backbone probing. MERT combines an acoustic teacher with a **musical teacher based on CQT**, scales from **95M to 330M** parameters, and reports strong performance across **14 music understanding tasks**. MusicFM is a music foundation model built around masked token modeling; its public implementation uses a **12-layer Conformer**, **24 kHz input**, and exposes `get_latent(layer_ix=7)` directly in the README, which is unusually convenient for layer selection experiments. MuQ uses **Mel-RVQ** targets, reports improvements over prior SSL music models, exposes hidden states in its public API, and the open checkpoint is roughly **300M** parameters. citeturn27search3turn15view1turn16view0turn6view3turn11view0turn2search1turn25view2turn25view3

For OBRUXO specifically, the key attraction of this family is not only benchmark strength. It is that these models were pretrained to encode **musically structured acoustic information** without requiring text prompts or explicit note labels at inference time. That is much closer to your setup than CLAP-style retrieval models or speech SSL encoders. Recent work probing MusicFM and MuQ also focuses specifically on **layer-wise specialization** and task-dependent layer choice, which is exactly the sort of flexibility you want when searching for a timbre-heavy but performance-light latent. citeturn17view0turn17view2turn17view3turn17view4

### General-audio baselines that are still worth keeping

A second family is the “general audio, practical baseline” group: **HTS-AT**, **PANNs/CNN14**, and, with more caution, **BEATs** or **AudioMAE**. HTS-AT is attractive because it is explicitly positioned as an **efficient, light-weight** audio transformer with **30M parameters**, yet it reaches very strong AudioSet and ESC-50 results. PANNs/CNN14 remains a durable baseline because it is simple, well-used, pretrained on AudioSet, easy to fine-tune, and has transfer examples even for music classification. BEATs is stronger semantically than older AudioSet models and exposes clean `extract_features` usage, but its paper also explicitly frames its discrete-label training as encouraging the model to keep **high-level audio semantics** and discard **redundant details**; that may or may not be what you want when the target is fine-grained synth timbre rather than coarse event identity. AudioMAE is a broad masked-audio-representation option with released checkpoints, but its surrounding tooling is less obviously convenient for a fast POC than the music-native options above. citeturn26view0turn8view0turn1search2turn10view0turn26view1turn13search6

For OBRUXO, these are best treated as **control baselines**, not likely winners. If a lightweight general-audio encoder matches a heavier music-specific model on your patch-identity experiments, that is extremely informative: it means you do not need the extra complexity. But if it loses badly on held-out-performance patch retrieval or controllable timbre probes, you learn that music-native pretraining is genuinely buying you something relevant. citeturn16view1turn27search0

### Families to de-prioritize in the first pass

The first family to de-prioritize is **highly specialized performance encoders** such as pitch- or note-centric models. The repo’s own framing already assumes that internally derived performance features can come from a frozen auxiliary model, while the encoder research question is about a separate representation that should stabilize synthesis identity across performances. In other words, the specialized “what notes are being played?” problem is already accounted for elsewhere in the architecture, and duplicating it in the timbre trunk makes disentanglement harder, not easier. citeturn0view0turn0view1

The second family to de-prioritize is **multimodal audio-text or instruction-tuned audio towers**. MARBLE now supports encoders such as Qwen2-Audio and Qwen2.5-Omni, but their stated purpose is broader multimodal interaction and instruction-following rather than compact, local, frozen feature extraction for synthesis inversion. Since OBRUXO has **zero text-conditioning requirement** in the current research tracker, those towers add irrelevant objectives and likely unnecessary runtime cost. citeturn19view0turn21view1

The third family to park for later is **neural audio codec encoders** such as EnCodec or DAC. They are excellent if your goal is to preserve the waveform faithfully; that is literally what their papers and repos optimize for. But for OBRUXO, “preserve everything” is not automatically good, because a high-fidelity codec latent will also preserve note content, articulation, and performance details that you are explicitly trying to factor away from synthesis identity. That does not make codec latents useless, but it makes them better as later ablations or auxiliary teachers than as the initial timbre encoder. This is an inference from the codec objective, not a direct claim from OBRUXO’s repo. citeturn14search0turn14search1turn0view0

## Best candidates for a first OBRUXO POC

My recommendation under your constraints is to treat the shortlist in four tiers: **MusicFM-MSD first, MERT-v1-95M second, HTS-AT or PANNs as the lightweight control, and MuQ as the stretch-quality option**. That ordering is not a claim that MusicFM is universally “best” in MIR. It is a claim about the balance of **simplicity, layer access, music-domain relevance, and expected local runtime practicality** for this exact problem. citeturn6view3turn27search3turn25view2turn26view0turn8view0

**MusicFM-MSD is the most attractive first POC encoder.** The repo is unusually direct about how to use it, the public code exposes per-layer latents out of the box, the architecture is a relatively understandable **12-layer Conformer**, and the implementation runs at a **25 Hz latent rate** from **24 kHz audio**. That lower temporal token rate is a real practical advantage for CPU-ish local inference, because it shrinks the sequence the adapter has to process. The public README also explicitly notes that the **MSD version performs better than the FMA version**. My biggest caveat is that MusicFM has less broad community usage than older baselines and does not have the same weight of published results as MERT or MuQ, but for a clean, fast timbre-stack prototype, it is arguably the best mix of domain fit and simplicity. The inference-speed part of that judgment is my inference from the published architecture rather than a reported benchmark. citeturn6view3turn11view0turn16view0

**MERT-v1-95M is the strongest second candidate and probably the safest overall choice if you want more evidence behind the model.** MERT is squarely music-native, explicitly uses a **musical CQT teacher** in addition to acoustic pseudo-labels, reports strong overall MIR performance, and has an official Hugging Face interface plus a clearly documented **95M** checkpoint. Compared with MusicFM, I expect MERT to encode more performance information by default, not less, because its design is deeply aware of tonal content and uses HuBERT-style feature extraction with additional CQT structure. That is not fatal for OBRUXO; it just means the adapter will likely need stronger invariance training. If you want one encoder that is likely to work well even before clever adapter training, MERT-v1-95M is arguably the safest bet. citeturn27search3turn15view1turn23search3

**HTS-AT or PANNs should be your lightweight control baseline.** If you prefer a transformer-shaped baseline, start with HTS-AT: the official repo emphasizes **30M parameters** and efficiency, which is a very reasonable fit for your local-use budget. If you prefer maximum simplicity and mature transfer-learning examples, use PANNs/CNN14: it is older, simpler, and easier to treat as a sanity-check encoder. I would not expect either to beat the top music-native models on the hardest “same patch across different performances” tests, but they are exactly the sort of baseline that can save months of overengineering if they turn out to be “good enough.” citeturn26view0turn8view0

**MuQ is the quality-first stretch option, not the first thing I would wire into the local CPU POC.** The reasons to like it are strong: it is music-native, its authors report state-of-the-art MIR performance, it exposes hidden states cleanly, and it is the most natural candidate if you later optimize for absolute representation quality rather than simplicity. The reason to hold it back at first is equally straightforward: the open checkpoint is roughly **300M parameters**, the repo says it requires **24 kHz input**, and it even recommends **fp32** inference to avoid NaN issues. For a POC that should feel reasonably local and musician-friendly, that profile makes MuQ more of a second-phase candidate than a first-phase default. citeturn2search1turn25view0turn25view2turn25view3

If you want the shortest possible actionable answer, it is this: start with **MusicFM-MSD**, reproduce everything with **MERT-v1-95M**, keep **HTS-AT or PANNs** as the “cheap honesty baseline,” and only bring in **MuQ** if the first two fail to keep patch identity stable across performance changes. citeturn6view3turn27search3turn26view0turn8view0turn25view2

## How to use these encoders without overcommitting to cross-attention

The repo already gives you permission not to over-index on cross-attention: it calls the fusion operator a working default rather than a major research axis. So for encoder research, I would not start with a complicated dual-stream fusion block at all. I would start with a **single frozen pretrained timbre encoder**, extract one or more layers, and train only a very small **adapter + pooling head** into a patch-level latent. That keeps the question clean: “Does this encoder contain a usable, performance-robust timbre signal at all?” citeturn0view0

A practical architecture is: frozen encoder → learnable layer mixer → lightweight temporal reducer → `z_timbre`. The temporal reducer can be as simple as **mean + std pooling**, attentive statistics pooling, or a 1–2 layer temporal transformer operating on already-downsampled encoder tokens. I would avoid immediately pooling to one vector from the final layer only. MusicFM’s public interface already encourages explicit layer selection, and the layer-wise MusicFM/MuQ analysis paper exists for a reason: different layers specialize differently, so a learned layer mixture is a much safer default than “take the last hidden state.” citeturn6view3turn17view0turn17view2turn25view3

If you later keep a separate performance branch, I would treat the performance signal as a **nuisance variable you condition against**, not as something that must be fused through cross-attention. In early experiments, alternatives like late concatenation, FiLM-style conditioning, residual subtraction, or even adversarial nuisance removal are all fair game, because the repo explicitly says fusion variants should not become a major ablation matrix unless they prove to be a bottleneck. citeturn0view0

## How to train the timbre adapter before the decoder exists

This is where OBRUXO’s synthetic setup is actually a strength. Because the project can generate multiple renderings of the **same patch** under different note sequences and performance styles, you can train an encoder adapter **without any patch decoder at all**. The repo itself already points toward self-supervised objectives built around same-patch/different-performance stability, and it names contrastive learning and JEPA-style prediction as leading families. citeturn0view0

My default first experiment would be: freeze the pretrained encoder, learn a small adapter into `z_timbre`, and train it with **same-patch / different-performance positives** and **different-patch negatives**. A SimCLR-style objective gives you the simplest starting point, and adding VICReg-style variance/covariance regularization is a clean way to reduce collapse risk without making the setup much more complicated. If you want one sentence summary: make the latent stable across performance changes, but keep it information-rich enough that it still predicts patch identity and coarse synth properties. citeturn28search0turn28search1turn0view0

A particularly important negative-sampling trick for this domain is to use **different patches rendered with the same or very similar MIDI** as hard negatives. That removes the model’s easiest shortcut. If two clips share note sequence, rhythm, and contour but come from different presets, then the only robust way to separate them is timbre. This is not a quoted recipe from the repo; it is the most direct operationalization of the repo’s requirement that the model recover synthesis identity rather than the observed notes. citeturn0view0

I would also add a **gradient-reversal or adversarial leakage head** trained to reconstruct note or MIDI features from `z_timbre`, while the adapter learns to make that probe fail. Since the repo already allows a separate frozen performance model, you have a natural control: the performance branch should perform well on note reconstruction, while the timbre branch should perform poorly. This is a much sharper test than just hoping the contrastive loss will implicitly factor notes away. citeturn0view0turn0view1

Finally, use **coarse auxiliary supervision** where it is honest and cheap. The research tracker explicitly permits source-parameter supervision as an auxiliary signal for identifiable controls. So if synthetic data gives you known oscillator category, filter type, effect enables, envelope regime, and patch family tags, use shallow auxiliary heads on top of `z_timbre`. That helps prevent a degenerate latent that is invariant but useless. citeturn0view0

## How to evaluate timbre capture without the full decoder

Your idea that the timbre latent should do **bad note reconstruction** is good, but it is only a **negative** test. A completely collapsed latent would also reconstruct notes badly. So the evaluation has to combine **bad note recovery** with **good patch/timbre recovery**. citeturn0view0

The best way to structure this, in my view, is to copy the logic already used in the repo’s LFO representation experiment: first evaluate a **representation ceiling** before investing in the final model. That experiment explicitly asks what a compact representation could achieve under an oracle encoder before testing full inferability from audio. The same logic transfers nicely here: first ask whether a shallow probe can recover patch-relevant information from the frozen-encoder-based latent at all; only after that should you spend time on the full OBRUXO decoder. citeturn24view1

A good encoder-only evaluation suite would have four parts. First, **patch retrieval under performance variation**: given one clip, retrieve other clips from the same patch but different performances, and measure Recall@K or mAP. Second, **note leakage probes**: train a small head to predict note onsets, framewise pitch bins, or simple MIDI attributes from `z_timbre`; you want this to underperform the performance branch badly. Third, **coarse timbre probes**: train shallow heads for oscillator family, filter type, envelope regime, effect enables, or patch-family tags. Fourth, **distance-structure tests**: same-patch / different-performance pairs should be closer in latent space than same-performance / different-patch pairs. All four tests are directly downstream of the repo’s “synthesis identity over performance” goal. citeturn0view0turn0view1

I would also compute a simple **invariance matrix** on synthetic data: for each latent, compare variance explained by patch ID versus variance explained by performance metadata. If patch ID is weak and performance is strong, the encoder is wrong for this task. If both are weak, the adapter has collapsed. If patch ID is strong and performance weak, you are close to the desired operating point. That is an inference-based metric design, but it follows directly from the project’s stated objective. citeturn0view0

If you want one especially practical “go / no-go” criterion before decoder work, use this: a candidate timbre stack is worth keeping only if it achieves **high same-patch retrieval across held-out performances**, **predicts coarse synth properties well**, and **fails note reconstruction relative to the frozen performance baseline**. If it cannot do those three things simultaneously, it is not yet a useful OBRUXO timbre representation. citeturn0view0turn24view1

## Recommendation

For the first serious OBRUXO encoder contribution, I would build the workstream around **MusicFM-MSD and MERT-v1-95M**. Those two give you the best trade: both are music-native and frozen-friendly, MusicFM is especially attractive for straightforward layer experiments and likely lower sequence cost, while MERT is the most evidence-backed “safe” music encoder in the shortlist. Keep **HTS-AT or PANNs** in the loop as a lightweight honesty baseline, and treat **MuQ** as the stretch encoder for phase two if the first pair cannot hold patch identity stable enough. citeturn6view3turn27search3turn26view0turn8view0turn25view2

If I had to make the plan maximally concrete, it would be this. First, freeze **MusicFM-MSD**, learn a tiny layer-mixing + stats-pooling adapter, and run only encoder-side retrieval and leakage probes. Second, do the same with **MERT-v1-95M** on the identical splits. Third, add **HTS-AT or PANNs** to establish a lightweight baseline. Fourth, only if those results suggest a clear ceiling, bring in **MuQ**. This sequence keeps the engineering simple, keeps the question focused on the timbre stack, and gives you a defensible path from “frozen representations” to “decoder-worthy latent.” citeturn6view3turn15view1turn26view0turn8view0turn25view2turn24view1
